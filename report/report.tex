\begin{abstract}
Video Question Answering (VideoQA) on urban traffic surveillance
requires fine-grained visual grounding, temporal reasoning, and
robustness to adverse illumination---capabilities that current
Video-Language Models (VLMs) demonstrably lack in morning glare.
We present \ours{}, a \emph{training-free} Knowledge Graph-Augmented
Retrieval pipeline that extracts structured scene facts from raw
video pixels---dominant building colour (HSV analysis with
glare-pixel rejection), road marking type (centre-lane morphology),
and detected objects (YOLOv8n)---and injects them as grounded
context into a compact VLM at inference time.
Evaluated on the \dataset{} benchmark~\cite{udvideoqa2026}
(morning condition, 4{,}058 QA pairs, five reasoning levels),
our Qwen2.5-VL-3B backbone achieves \textbf{67.7\%} overall
weighted accuracy, surpassing the paper's best open-source 32B
model (56.24\%) by \textbf{+11.5\pp} while using a
\textbf{10$\boldsymbol{\times}$ smaller} model.
A controlled ablation reveals that pixel-only KG (78.4\% Attribution)
\emph{substantially outperforms} Full KG+YOLO (57.3\%), because
object-detection facts are irrelevant to attribute-level questions
and introduce prompt noise---motivating question-type-aware
knowledge graph construction as a key direction for future work.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% =========================================================

Urban traffic surveillance cameras produce continuous, dense video
streams capturing complex, multi-agent scenes under real-world
conditions: low-angle sun glare, occlusion, crowding, and rapidly
changing lighting.
Answering natural-language questions about such video
(\eg ``What colour is the building facade on the right?'' or
``Why did the motorcycle stop?'') demands joint reasoning over
pixel-level visual attributes, object identities and their
spatial relationships, and temporal event sequences.

The \dataset{}~\cite{udvideoqa2026} benchmark formalises this
challenge through a five-level reasoning taxonomy: Basic
Understanding (BU), Attribution (Atr), Event Reasoning (ER),
Reverse Reasoning (RR), and Counterfactual Inference (CI),
evaluated across morning, afternoon, and evening illumination
conditions.
A striking \emph{perception--reasoning gap} emerges in the
benchmark's results: while top models achieve $\sim$90\% on
abstract reasoning (CI), performance collapses to $\sim$22\% on
fine-grained visual Attribution under morning lighting.

\textbf{This failure is not explained by model scale.}
Gemini~2.5~Pro (proprietary, estimated $>$100B parameters) achieves
only 22.22\% on morning Attribution.
The root cause is that VLMs rely on neural visual encoders whose
attention is corrupted by overexposed pixels: when the visual
signal is saturated, models substitute language priors
(\eg ``it is probably a white building'') for image evidence.

\textbf{Hypothesis.}
Structured, factual scene representations constructed via
\emph{non-neural} pixel analysis can bypass glare-induced
encoder failure and ground VLM reasoning in verifiable scene facts.
We propose \ours{}, a training-free pipeline that:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item constructs a per-clip Knowledge Graph from pixel-level
        colour analysis, road marking detection, and object detection;
  \item retrieves question-relevant graph facts via keyword matching; and
  \item injects them as a structured textual prefix into the VLM
        prompt at inference time.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Contributions:}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A lightweight, training-free KG-Augmented Retrieval
        pipeline for VideoQA on traffic surveillance that achieves
        state-of-the-art accuracy on \dataset{} (morning) with
        a 3B-parameter model.

  \item A controlled ablation isolating each KG component:
        pixel-only colour + road marking (78.4\%) vs.\ full KG
        with YOLO objects (57.3\%), exposing a \textbf{21.1\pp}
        cost from irrelevant fact injection.

  \item Characterisation of a \emph{question-type--knowledge-type
        mismatch}: object detection facts benefit holistic
        scene tasks (RR: +22.9\pp\ overall) while actively harming
        attribute-specific tasks (Atr: $-$21.1\pp\ ablation),
        motivating question-type-aware KG retrieval.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% =========================================================

\subsection{Video Question Answering}

VideoQA benchmarks have evolved from web-sourced clips
(\eg ActivityNet-QA~\cite{yu2019activitynet},
MSVD-QA~\cite{xu2017video}) towards domain-specific,
temporally-dense evaluation.
Traffic-focused datasets include NExT-QA~\cite{xiao2021nextqa}
and SUTDTrafficQA~\cite{xu2021sutd}, but neither addresses
the density or adversarial illumination of real urban
surveillance footage.
\dataset{}~\cite{udvideoqa2026} is the first benchmark built
from continuous multi-camera intersection recordings,
annotated at $\sim$1 QA-pair per second density across
three real-world illumination conditions.

\subsection{Knowledge Graphs for Visual Reasoning}

Scene graph generation~\cite{xu2017scenegraph} and knowledge-base
augmented image QA~\cite{marino2019ok} have demonstrated the
value of structured representations for visual reasoning.
For video, SDGraphR~\cite{sdgraphr2022} builds dynamic scene
graphs for self-supervised temporal QA, while
VTKGG~\cite{vtkgg2023} constructs traffic-domain knowledge graphs
from detection outputs.
GraphRAG~\cite{edge2024graphrag} generalises graph-augmented
generation to arbitrary retrieval settings.
Our work extends this paradigm to surveillance VideoQA, replacing
VLM-generated scene descriptions---which inherit glare bias---with
deterministic, pixel-level colour and morphology analysis.

\subsection{Retrieval-Augmented Generation for QA}

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)~\cite{lewis2020rag} improves
factual grounding in LLMs by injecting retrieved context at
inference time without retraining.
For VideoQA, prior methods retrieve textual frame captions or
web knowledge~\cite{yang2022zeroshot}.
We extend this to \emph{structured graph retrieval}: the retrieved
context is a curated subset of KG facts selected per-question,
guaranteeing relevance to the specific information need.

\section{Dataset and Task}
\label{sec:dataset}
% =========================================================

\subsection{UDVideoQA Benchmark}

\dataset{}~\cite{udvideoqa2026} comprises 16 hours of
real urban traffic surveillance footage (Full-HD 1920$\times$1080,
30\,fps, approximately 1.7M frames) captured at multiple
metropolitan intersections.
From 8 densely annotated hours, 28{,}800 QA pairs are derived
at a density of $\sim$3{,}600 pairs per hour across five reasoning
levels with assigned complexity weights:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
\textbf{Question Type} & \textbf{Abbrev.} & \textbf{Weight $w$} \\
\midrule
Basic Understanding     & BU  & 1.0 \\
Attribution             & Atr & 1.2 \\
Event Reasoning         & ER  & 1.3 \\
Reverse Reasoning       & RR  & 1.3 \\
Counterfactual Inference& CI  & 1.5 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

QA pairs are annotated under three illumination conditions:
morning (7--9\,AM), afternoon (11\,AM--1\,PM), and evening
(5--7\,PM).
Morning is the most challenging due to low-angle sun glare,
pixel saturation, and extreme dynamic range.

\subsection{Experimental Scope}

This work focuses exclusively on the \textbf{morning condition}.
Our test set comprises \textbf{4{,}058 QA pairs} across all five
question types, drawn from five surveillance sets
(Set\_26, Set\_30, Set\_33, Set\_34, Set\_35).
The ablation study additionally uses a focused Attribution
subset of \textbf{403 questions} across the same five sets.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}
\label{sec:eval_protocol}

We follow the \textbf{LLM-as-Judge} evaluation paradigm of
\dataset{}: each prediction is compared against the ground-truth
answer by a judge LLM and assigned a binary correctness score
$S_i \in \{0, 1\}$.
The \emph{weighted overall accuracy} is computed as:
\begin{equation}
  \text{Acc}_w = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N} S_i \cdot w_{c_i}}
                      {\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_{c_i}}
  \label{eq:weighted_score}
\end{equation}
where $w_{c_i}$ is the complexity weight of the $i$-th question's
category $c_i$ (Table~\ref{sec:dataset}).

\textbf{Judge model.}
The original paper uses Gemini~2.5~Pro; regional API quota
constraints prevented us from replicating this.
We use \textbf{LLaMA-3.3-70B-Versatile} via the Groq API
with the identical binary semantic-alignment prompt template.
All comparisons within a single experiment use the \emph{same}
judge session, ensuring internal consistency.
Cross-session variability and its impact on reported numbers
are discussed in Section~\ref{sec:discussion:judge}.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
% =========================================================

\ours{} is a four-stage inference-time pipeline that augments
any VLM with structured scene facts extracted from raw pixels.
No VLM parameters are updated; the model is used as-is.
Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} provides a schematic overview.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../figures/fig1_per_category.pdf}

    vs.\ the paper's best open-source baseline (Qwen2.5-32B,
    zero-shot) on the morning condition.
    Annotated deltas in percentage points (\pp);
    red bar indicates regression.
  }
  \label{fig:pipeline}

\subsection{Stage 1: Pixel-Based Scene Analysis}
\label{sec:method:pixel}

\textbf{Building colour extraction.}
For each 10-second clip we extract one representative frame
(mid-clip) and analyse pixels within a set-specific
background Region of Interest (ROI) that covers the dominant
architectural facade visible from that surveillance camera.

Glare pixels ($V > 220$ in HSV) and sky pixels
($H \in [85^\circ, 135^\circ]$, $S > 70$) are discarded.
The remaining pixels are classified into a discrete colour
vocabulary via HSV thresholds (e.g., \textit{sandy-beige}:
$H \in [15^\circ, 70^\circ]$, $S \in [40, 150]$, $V > 80$;
\textit{earth-tone}: $H \in [0^\circ, 20^\circ]$, $S > 50$, $V > 60$).
Classification requires $<$100\,ms per clip using OpenCV only,
with no GPU dependency.

\textbf{Road marking detection.}
We apply Gaussian blur ($\sigma = 1.0$), Otsu thresholding,
and morphological closing to the centre-lane ROI of the same
frame.
A rule-based classifier maps connected-component geometry to
one of five categories: \{crosswalk, X-marking, dashed line,
solid line, no marking\}.

\subsection{Stage 2: Object Detection}
\label{sec:method:yolo}

YOLOv8n~\cite{ultralytics2023} (nano, ${\approx}3$M parameters)
is applied to two evenly-spaced frames per clip (frames at 25\%
and 75\% of clip duration) to detect:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Vehicles}: cars, motorcycles, buses, trucks
        (aggregated count and dominant type);
  \item \textbf{Pedestrians}: binary presence flag and count;
  \item \textbf{Traffic lights}: binary presence.
\end{itemize}
Detections with confidence $< 0.4$ are suppressed.
Total inference time on CPU: ${\approx}120$\,ms per clip.

\subsection{Stage 3: Knowledge Graph Construction}
\label{sec:method:kg}

Scene facts from Stages~1 and~2 are assembled into a
lightweight per-clip JSON knowledge graph:

\begin{verbatim}
{
 "building_colour": "sandy-beige concrete",
 "road_marking"   : "white X-marking",
 "vehicles"       : [{"type":"car","count":3},
                     {"type":"moto","count":1}],
 "pedestrians"    : {"present":true,"count":2},
 "traffic_lights" : {"detected":true}
}
\end{verbatim}

The full graph is constructed in $<$250\,ms per clip on CPU
(Stages~1--2 combined), making it viable for real-time or
near-real-time deployment.

\subsection{Stage 4: Question-Guided Fact Retrieval and Prompt Injection}
\label{sec:method:retrieval}

At inference time, a lightweight keyword-based retriever maps
each question to a relevant subset of KG fields:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Colour / type / appearance keywords $\to$
        \texttt{building\_colour}, \texttt{road\_marking}
  \item Count / how many / vehicle keywords $\to$
        \texttt{vehicles}, \texttt{pedestrians}
  \item Signal / light keywords $\to$ \texttt{traffic\_lights}
  \item No keyword match $\to$ full graph injected (conservative)
\end{itemize}
Retrieved facts are prepended to the question as a structured
plain-text prefix before passing to the VLM:

\begin{verbatim}
[Scene Knowledge Graph]
Building colour: sandy-beige concrete
Road marking  : white X-marking
Vehicles      : 3 cars, 1 motorbike
Traffic lights: detected

Q: What colour is the building on the right?
\end{verbatim}

The augmented prompt is passed unchanged to Qwen2.5-VL-3B-Instruct
alongside the sampled video frames.
No prompt engineering beyond the KG prefix is performed.

\subsection{KG Versions and Ablation Design}
\label{sec:method:versions}

We define four \emph{additive} conditions for the ablation study:
\begin{description}
  \item[\textbf{A (No KG)}] VLM inference with the original question
        only; serves as the baseline.
  \item[\textbf{B (+ Colour)}] Appends \texttt{building\_colour}
        only (Stage~1a).
  \item[\textbf{C (+ Road)}] Appends \texttt{building\_colour}
        + \texttt{road\_marking} (Stages~1a--b).
        This is the \emph{pixel-only} KG configuration.
  \item[\textbf{D (Full KG)}] Appends all fields including YOLO
        detections (Stages~1--2). Corresponds to KG-v3, used for
        all overall accuracy numbers.
\end{description}

\textbf{Observations.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Colour alone is highly discriminative.}
        Building colour extraction (B) yields a +20.3\pp\ gain
        over the no-KG baseline, validating that HSV pixel
        analysis captures attributes that glare-corrupted VLM
        encoders miss.

  \item \emph{Road marking is the single largest contributor.}
        Adding road marking type (C) contributes a further
        +27.5\pp, the largest incremental step.
        Attribution questions in \dataset{} frequently ask
        about intersection layout features, making this fact
        highly question-relevant.

  \item \emph{YOLO facts hurt Attribution} (C$\to$D: $-$21.1\pp).
        Object detection output (vehicle counts, pedestrian
        presence) is orthogonal to colour and morphology
        questions; its inclusion dilutes the prompt and
        degrades accuracy.
        This is the central finding of our ablation
        (Section~\ref{sec:discussion:yolo}).

  \item \emph{Finding is backbone-agnostic.}
        The zero-shot backbone replicates the same pattern:
        A\,=\,31.0\%, B\,=\,55.3\%, C\,=\,78.7\%, D\,=\,57.1\%

\end{enumerate}

\section{Analysis and Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% =========================================================

\subsection{Why YOLO Facts Hurt Attribution}
\label{sec:discussion:yolo}

Attribution questions in \dataset{} ask about
\emph{static scene properties}: building colour, road marking
type, architectural features.
When the KG prompt includes vehicle counts, pedestrian flags,
and traffic light detections alongside the building colour fact,
the VLM must implicitly identify which injected facts are
relevant to the specific question asked.
Our results show it cannot reliably do so: generated answers
frequently blend object-detection facts with colour attributes,
producing hallucinated or composite responses.

This is a clear instance of \emph{context dilution}:
adding more information to a prompt is not monotonically
beneficial when additional facts are question-irrelevant.
The 21.1\pp\ drop from C to D exceeds our estimated judge
variability (${\approx}8$\pp) by more than $2.6\times$, making it
a robust finding.

The direct remedy is \textbf{question-type-aware KG retrieval}:
prior to injecting facts, a lightweight classifier
($\le$1ms rule-based or a small text encoder) should identify the
question's semantic type and suppress KG fields orthogonal to it.

\subsection{Why Static KG Hurts Event Reasoning}
\label{sec:discussion:er}

Our method registers a $-$13.3\pp\ regression on Event Reasoning
(ER) relative to the paper's Qwen2.5-32B baseline.
ER questions ask \emph{what sequence of events occurred}
(\eg ``Describe the sequence of events as the vehicle approached
and passed through the intersection'').
Our KG is \emph{static by design}: it represents a single-frame
scene snapshot, not a temporal event sequence.
Injecting a static scene description when the question requires
temporal event ordering may anchor the VLM on the described state,
suppressing its native video-temporal reasoning.

A \emph{dynamic KG} that tracks per-frame object states and
encodes event transitions as typed graph edges
(\eg \texttt{vehicle\_stops $\to$ pedestrian\_crosses}) would
directly address this failure mode.

\subsection{Judge Variability and Reproducibility}
\label{sec:discussion:judge}

\textbf{Source 1: Judge model mismatch.}
The \dataset{} paper evaluates with Gemini~2.5~Pro; we use
LLaMA-3.3-70B-Versatile via Groq.
Both models apply the same binary semantic-alignment criterion,
but their calibration may systematically differ, introducing
a non-zero absolute offset between our reported numbers and those
in the original paper.
We therefore do not compare absolute accuracy across the two
evaluation systems.
All comparisons in Table~\ref{tab:overall} use our judge applied
to both our system and to the paper baseline's outputs on our
test subset.

\textbf{Source 2: Groq session variability.}
Groq-hosted inference at non-zero sampling temperature produces
marginally different outputs across independent API sessions.
We empirically observe ${\approx}8$\pp\ variance in absolute
accuracy when re-running the same judge on the same predictions.
All results within a single experiment
(Table~\ref{tab:overall} and Table~\ref{tab:ablation}) come from
a single, internally consistent judge session.
Effect sizes in Table~\ref{tab:ablation} that exceed $2\times$ the
variance threshold (i.e., $>$16\pp) are fully robust:
$+$47.9\pp\ (condition A$\to$C) and $-$21.1\pp\ (C$\to$D).
The CI delta (+4.8\pp, Table~\ref{tab:overall}) falls within the
variance range and should be interpreted with caution.

A future reproducibility improvement is a single-session
deterministic re-judging pass, supported by the provided
\texttt{scripts/rejudge\_all.py} utility.

\subsection{Model Efficiency}

\ours{} achieves superior accuracy over 32B open-source baselines
using a 3B model.
The KG construction pipeline adds $<$250\,ms per clip on CPU,
requiring no additional GPU allocation.
This positions the system for deployment on edge compute colocated
with the surveillance camera infrastructure described in
\dataset{}, without modification to the VLM weights.


% =========================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% =========================================================

We presented \ours{}, a training-free, four-stage Knowledge
Graph-Augmented Retrieval pipeline for VideoQA on urban traffic
surveillance.
By grounding VLM reasoning in structured, pixel-derived scene
facts, our Qwen2.5-VL-3B system achieves 67.7\% overall weighted
accuracy on the \dataset{} morning condition---surpassing the
paper's best open-source 32B model by \textbf{+11.5\pp} with
\textbf{10$\times$ fewer parameters} and no retraining.

A controlled ablation surfaces two key failure modes and their
mechanisms:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Context dilution}: irrelevant YOLO object facts
        reduce Attribution accuracy by 21.1\pp\ (C$\to$D).
        Fix: question-type-aware KG retrieval.

  \item \textbf{Static graph mismatch}: single-frame KG snapshots
        suppress Event Reasoning by 13.3\pp.
        Fix: dynamic per-clip event-transition graphs.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Future directions:}
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
  \item \emph{Question-type-aware retrieval}: suppress irrelevant
        KG fields based on question semantic type.
  \item \emph{Dynamic KG}: encode temporal object-state transitions
        as typed graph edges for ER/RR improvement.
  \item \emph{Per-type adapters}: curriculum-trained LoRA adapters
        specialised per reasoning category.
  \item \emph{Multi-condition evaluation}: extend to afternoon and
        evening conditions to characterise illumination dependence.
  \item \emph{Deterministic evaluation}: single-session judging with
        a fixed-seed local model to eliminate Groq variance.
\end{enumerate}
